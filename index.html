Neural Noir

A body in the study, a houseful of suspects, and a detective who never runs out of patience for your questions. Neural Noir is an AI detective game where a language model runs the whole mystery — generating the case, role-playing every suspect, and holding a hidden, consistent truth you have to uncover. I rebuilt it from scratch because making an LLM keep a secret and never contradict itself is a genuine reasoning challenge.

What it is

A mystery with a mind behind it

Neural Noir hands the role of game master to a language model. It invents a crime — a culprit, a motive, a tangle of alibis — and then lets you interrogate the suspects, each voiced by the model in character. You ask questions, catch contradictions, follow leads, and finally name who did it. The fun is that the case is fresh every time and the suspects answer in natural language.

The hard engineering is keeping it honest. The model must hold a fixed underlying truth, never accidentally reveal the culprit, never contradict an earlier answer, and let innocent suspects lie or mislead plausibly. That's a problem of state, memory and carefully bounded prompting — the real substance under the storytelling.

Architecture

How a case plays out

The model runs the mystery while a thin layer keeps it honest:

  1. Generate

    Create a consistent case and lock in the hidden solution.

  2. Interrogate

    The player questions suspects, each answered in character.

  3. Constrain

    Prompts and stored state keep answers consistent and the culprit concealed.

  4. Deduce

    The player pieces together contradictions and clues.

  5. Accuse

    The final guess is checked against the hidden truth.

Reflection

What rebuilding it taught me

  • Consistency is the hard part. Generating a story is easy; keeping a hidden truth intact across a long interrogation is the real work.
  • State lives outside the model. The reliable truth has to be stored and managed by your code — you can't trust the model to just remember it.
  • Prompts are game rules. What a suspect can and can't say is enforced by prompt design — that's where the game balance lives.
  • Plausible lying is a feature. Innocent suspects must mislead believably without breaking the fixed facts — a delicate prompting line.
  • LLMs are great game masters. Open-ended, in-character, infinitely patient narration is something only a language model makes easy.
